Portsmouth is England's most densely populated city

Portsmouth has 5145 people / km2

London has 4984 people / km2

Source: Nomis

The high population-density of Portsmouth means there are many vulnerable road users in a small area

Vulnerable road users include
pedestrians, cyclists, motorcyclists and horseriders

Source: The Highway Code

373 of Portsmouth's vulnerable road users became road casualties during 2014

One casualty for every day of the year
and a few spare
including:

  • 71 motorcyclists
  • 105 pedestrians
  • 197 cyclists

Road casualty figures only include those reported to the police, so these figures do not include all casualties from all incidents

Source: RRC2014

71 of Portsmouth's
373 road vulnerable road user casualties in 2014 involved fatal or serious injury

That's nearly six of us every month

One every five days

  • 23 motorcyclists
  • 21 pedestrians
  • 34 cyclists

Source: RRC2014

The UK Government defines
serious injury as:

  • Fractures
  • Concussion
  • Internal injuries
  • Crushings
  • Burns
  • Severe cuts
  • Shock requiring medical treatment
  • Anything requiring hospitalisation
  • Injuries causing death 30+ days later

Source: UK Gov Definitions

Most cyclists do not jump red traffic lights

A Transport for London study in June 2007 concluded that 84% of cyclists that were observed obeyed the lights

A cyclist that jumps a read light (RLJ) is just as likely to hurt themselves as they are other vulnerable road users, so when it does happen it tends to be at a low speed. Compare this with the vehicle traveling at high speed, where the driver puts their foot down to beat the lights and fails

Source: TfL RNPR 8

The cyclists killed on Portsmouth's roads are just like you

On Portsea Island there have been 21 cycling fatalities since 1985

These are not young kamikaze no-handed headphone-wearing phone-using foolish cyclists: just normal people

  • The youngest was 13
  • The eldest was 87
  • The average age is 46
  • The median age is 33

Source: ICycleLiverpool

The age of the private car
is coming to and end

It's estimated that around 1900 there were only 8000 cars in the whole of Britain: in 2013 there were 35 million.

The roads have evolved, but are reaching their limit.

Source: 20th Century London, DfT Stats 2013

Cyclists are more likely
to have access to a car
than the general public

  • 83% of cyclists reside in a car-or-van owning household
  • Only 82% of the general public can say the same

So when you see someone on a bike, they likelihood is that they made a choice not to use their car or van: reducing traffic, reducing pollution, reducing the UKs reliance on imported energy and leaving parking spaces free for people who need them.

Source: National Travel Survey 08/09
